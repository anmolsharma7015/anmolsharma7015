# Case Study: UPI / Online Payment Fraud

Crime Type	UPI / Online Payment Fraud
Target Profile	First-year college students in India
Primary Platform	WhatsApp, Instagram DMs
Typical Loss	Rs. 500 – Rs. 5,000 per incident
Report Channel	cybercrime.gov.in  |  Helpline: 1930


# How It Happened — Step by Step
Priya, a first-year B.Tech student at a college in Pune, received a WhatsApp message in August 2024 from an unknown number with a profile picture showing a college ID card. The sender claimed to be a third-year senior selling barely used textbooks at 60% of the original price, attaching a professionally designed poster with real book cover images sourced online.
Step 1 — Trust building: The sender mentioned specific professors and first-year course codes (information easily found on public college websites), making the message feel legitimate.
Step 2 — Artificial urgency: The sender said 'only 2 copies left' and that another student was already interested, pressuring Priya to decide quickly.
Step 3 — Payment request: They asked for Rs. 950 as an advance via PhonePe 'to hold the books' before physical handover.
Step 4 — Disappearance: Immediately after Priya transferred the money, the number stopped responding and was soon blocked. The profile picture was removed.
Consequence: Priya lost Rs. 950 with no way to recover it. UPI transactions cannot be reversed without receiver cooperation. The registered SIM turned out to be temporary. She filed a report on cybercrime.gov.in but no recovery was possible. Beyond the financial loss, she experienced lasting distrust of online communications.

# Stay Safe Online: Prevention Checklist
This checklist is also saved as prevention-checklist.md in task-5-cybercrime/.

✓/✗	Rule
✓	Never send UPI payments to anyone you have not met in person or verified through an official platform.
✓	Always verify a seller's identity through at least two independent channels before any transaction.
✓	Use your UPI app's 'Verify UPI ID' or 'Check Merchant' feature before sending money to an unfamiliar contact.
✓	Enable a daily UPI transaction cap in your bank app — most apps let you set a Rs. 2,000 limit for unknown contacts.
✓	Never click on shortened or suspicious links (bit.ly, tinyurl) received from unknown numbers — these often lead to phishing pages.
✓	Use two-factor authentication (2FA) on all important accounts: college email, Gmail, Instagram, LinkedIn.
✓	Never share your OTP with anyone — no bank, UPI app, government official, or 'senior student' will ever ask for it.
✓	If your phone is lost or stolen, immediately call your bank helpline to freeze UPI-linked accounts.
✓	Report any cybercrime — including attempted fraud — at the National Cyber Crime Portal: cybercrime.gov.in
✓	Save the Cyber Crime Helpline: 1930 (available 24x7 in India). Call immediately if fraud is in progress.


# Reflection 
What surprised me most while researching this topic was how precisely these scams are engineered. The fraudster in Priya's case knew exactly which textbooks first-year CSE students use, which professors are well-known, and that students are actively looking for discounts in August. That level of targeted research was genuinely unsettling — it meant this was not random spam but a deliberate, repeated scheme aimed at a specific group.
The one habit I will personally change as a result of this task: I will never send a UPI payment to anyone I have not spoken to on a verified call and confirmed through at least one official channel. No discount, deal, or deadline justifies transferring money to an unverified stranger. The 30 seconds it takes to verify could save Rs. 1,000 or more.
